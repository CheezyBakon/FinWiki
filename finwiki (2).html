BEGINNER'S GUIDE

How to Trade Stocks

A clear, step-by-step guide to buying your first stock — from opening an account to building a long-term portfolio.

The 7 Steps to Get Started
1 1. Open a Brokerage Account
▾

To buy stocks you need a brokerage account — think of it as a bank account specifically for investing. Popular online brokers include Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Robinhood. Most offer $0 commission trades today.

What to look for:

  • No account minimums
  • $0 commission per trade
  • Educational resources
  • Strong mobile app
  • SIPC insurance (protects your account up to $500,000)
Pro tip: Start with a well-established broker like Fidelity or Schwab rather than newer apps. They offer better research tools and customer support.
2 2. Fund Your Account
▾

Connect your bank account and transfer money. Most brokers allow you to start with any amount — even $1. You can buy fractional shares of expensive stocks like Amazon or Google.

How much to start with:

  • You can begin with $100–$500 to learn the basics
  • Don't invest money you'll need within 1–3 years
  • Keep 3–6 months of expenses as an emergency fund first
  • Never invest on credit or borrowed money as a beginner
Pro tip: Only invest money you can afford to leave untouched for at least 3–5 years. Markets go up and down — time in the market beats timing the market.
3 3. Research Before You Buy
▾

Before buying any stock, understand what you're buying. For individual stocks, research the company:

  • What does the company do? (Can you explain it simply?)
  • Is it profitable? (Check earnings, revenue growth)
  • How is it valued? (P/E ratio compared to peers)
  • What are the risks? (Competition, regulation, debt)
  • What's the business model? (How does it actually make money?)

Alternatively, skip stock-picking and buy a total market ETF or S&P 500 index fund — statistically beats most active investors over time.

Pro tip: Warren Buffett's rule: never invest in a business you don't understand. If you can't explain how a company makes money in one sentence, keep researching.
4 4. Place Your First Order
▾

When you're ready to buy, you'll choose an order type:

  • Market Order — Buys immediately at the current price. Best for liquid, large-cap stocks.
  • Limit Order — Sets the maximum price you'll pay. Your order only fills at your price or better.
  • Stop-Loss Order — Automatically sells if the stock falls to a set price, limiting your loss.

For most beginners, a limit order is recommended — it ensures you don't pay more than you intended during volatile moments.

Pro tip: Avoid placing market orders for thinly-traded stocks or during the first/last 15 minutes of the trading day when prices can be erratic.
5 5. Build a Diversified Portfolio
▾

Don't put all your money into one stock. Diversification reduces risk:

  • Across companies — Own 20+ stocks or use an index fund
  • Across sectors — Tech, healthcare, consumer goods, energy, etc.
  • Across asset classes — Mix stocks with bonds based on your age/risk tolerance
  • Across geographies — Some U.S., some international

A simple beginner portfolio: 80% S&P 500 index fund + 20% bond index fund. As you learn more, you can add individual stocks.

Pro tip: For most people, a simple 3-fund portfolio (U.S. stocks, international stocks, bonds) outperforms complex strategies with less stress and lower fees.
6 6. Invest Consistently Over Time
▾

The most powerful investing strategy is dollar-cost averaging — investing a fixed amount regularly (e.g., $200 every month) regardless of what the market is doing.

Why this works:

  • Removes the impossible task of market timing
  • You automatically buy more shares when prices are low
  • Builds discipline and a savings habit
  • Smooths out the volatility over time

Set up automatic monthly investments so you invest without thinking about it.

Pro tip: Time in the market beats timing the market. Missing just the 10 best trading days in a decade can cut your returns in half.
7 7. Monitor (But Don't Obsess)
▾

Review your portfolio quarterly, not daily. Checking your portfolio constantly leads to emotional, reactive decisions that hurt returns.

What to review:

  • Is your asset allocation still on target? Rebalance if it's drifted significantly.
  • Have there been major changes to any company you own?
  • Are you still on track for your goals?

What NOT to do:

  • Don't sell because of a short-term dip
  • Don't chase recent hot performers
  • Don't read financial news as a signal to trade
Pro tip: Studies show that investors who check their portfolios least frequently earn the highest returns. Set it, forget it, and let compounding work.
When to Buy or Sell a Stock

Knowing when to act is one of the hardest skills in investing. Use these signals as a framework, not rigid rules.

Signals to BUY

Strong Earnings Growth
Revenue and profit rising consistently year-over-year
Stock at a Discount
P/E ratio below industry average or historical norms
Above Moving Average
Price bouncing off the 50-day or 200-day moving average
Bullish Chart Pattern
Cup-and-handle, breakout above resistance, or golden cross

Signals to SELL

Fundamentals Deteriorate
Revenue declining, margins compressing, or debt rising rapidly
Overvalued vs. Peers
P/E ratio far above industry average with no clear growth justification
Stop-Loss Hit
Stock has fallen to your pre-set maximum loss level
Thesis is Broken
The original reason you bought it no longer applies

The Golden Rule of Selling: Before selling, ask yourself: "If I didn't own this stock already, would I buy it today?" If the answer is no, it might be time to sell. If yes, hold — emotions shouldn't drive the decision.

Reading Stock Charts

Charts are the language of the market. Here are the most common chart types traders use.

Candlestick Chart

The most widely used chart in trading. Each "candle" shows 4 key prices for a time period: Open, High, Low, Close. Green candles = price went up. Red candles = price went down.

Anatomy of a Candlestick

Bullish (Green)
Bearish (Red)
Body

The thick rectangle — the range between open and close price

Wick / Shadow

The thin lines above/below the body — the high and low extremes

Green Candle

Close price is HIGHER than open — buyers won the session

Red Candle

Close price is LOWER than open — sellers won the session

Doji

Open ≈ close — indecision, often signals a trend reversal

Engulfing

A candle whose body fully contains the previous candle — strong reversal signal

Common Patterns to Recognize
Bullish Uptrend
Green candles with higher highs and higher lows — a strong buying signal
Bearish Downtrend
Red candles with lower highs and lower lows — consider selling or staying out
Reversal Signal
Doji candles (open ≈ close) followed by a strong green engulfing candle — potential buy signal
Remember: Candlestick patterns are most reliable when confirmed by trading volume and used alongside other indicators. Never trade based on a single pattern alone.

Line Chart

The simplest chart — connects closing prices with a line. Best for seeing the overall trend at a glance. Most commonly used in financial news and apps like Google Finance. Hides intraday volatility, making trends cleaner and easier to read.

Best used for
Long-term trend analysis, comparing multiple stocks, casual overview of performance
Limitations
Hides intraday price action, gaps, and volatility — lacks detail for active traders

OHLC Bar Chart

Open-High-Low-Close bars. Each bar shows all 4 key prices as a vertical line with small horizontal ticks. The left tick = open price; right tick = close price. Predates candlesticks and is still widely used by professional traders on desktop platforms.

Best used for
Professional trading platforms, dense data display, when screen real estate is limited
Limitations
Harder to read quickly vs candlesticks — takes practice to spot patterns

Area Chart

A line chart with the area below the line filled in with color. Makes it easy to visualize the magnitude of price movement and momentum. Popular in portfolio trackers and brokerage apps for its clean, visual look.

Best used for
Portfolio performance dashboards, showing growth over time, visual presentations
Limitations
Decorative rather than analytical — doesn't add information beyond a line chart

Volume Chart

Shows how many shares were traded each day as bars below the price chart. High volume confirms a price move — a breakout on high volume is more reliable than on low volume. One of the most important secondary indicators traders use.

Best used for
Confirming breakouts, spotting institutional buying, validating trend strength
Limitations
Rarely used alone — most useful when combined with price charts to confirm signals
Essential Terms to Know
Market Order
Executes immediately at current price
Limit Order
Only executes at your specified price or better
Stop-Loss
Auto-sells to cap your losses at a set level
P/E Ratio
How much investors pay per dollar of earnings
Dividend
Cash payments a company makes to shareholders
ETF
A basket of stocks you can buy as one investment
Broker
Platform where you buy and sell stocks
Portfolio
Your full collection of investments

✗

Don't day-trade as a beginner — 80%+ of day traders lose money

✗

Avoid 'hot tips' on social media or from friends

✗

Never invest emergency fund money or money you'll need soon

✗

Be skeptical of any investment promising unusually high returns

✗

Options and leverage are advanced tools — avoid until you have experience
